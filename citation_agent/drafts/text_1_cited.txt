Fortunately, there are alternatives for our current technology can overcome its
limitations. The awareness of the looming limits has sparked a global 
initiative to create alternate device technologies. There are indeed concrete solutions and
paths of scientific innovation to have more efficient and less-energy consuming
nanodevices. Conventional semiconductor devices use exclusively the charge of electrons 
to generate, transmit and process information \cite{cite_3}. The electron is a funda-
mental particle that plays a crucial role in many physical phenomena, including
electricity and magnetism. In the field of spintronics, researchers strive to ex-
ploit the spin of electrons in order to develop new technologies and devices. For
example, by controlling the spin of electrons, it may be possible to create more
energy-efficient transistors, memory devices, and sensors \cite{cite_5}. 
The field of spin-tronics also holds promise for applications in quantum computing, 
where the spin of an electron can be used to represent a quantum bit, or qubit \cite{cite_7}. 
In addition to the spin, the electron has other properties that have been used in various technological applications,
such as the spin-orbit interaction which causes electrons to change their spin states and that can be exploited for
use in data storage and logic operations as well as other applications such as quantum computing \cite{cite_8}.
The discovery of graphene and other atomically-thin two dimensional mate-rials opens the path to effectively c
ontrol and tune the required properties for disruptive technologies \cite{cite_8}. Two dimensional materials 
such as graphene and transition metal dichalcogenides (Transition Metal Dichalcogenide (TMDC)s) 
feature another way to control the so-called valley degree of freedom electrons display due to 
the crystal structure of the material \cite{cite_12}. The valleys are characterized by different electron spin 
and angular momentum states, and can be used to encode and manipulate quantum information \cite{cite_13}. 
The valley degree of freedom can also be used to create new types of electronic and optoelectronic devices, 
such as valley transistors and valley-based sensors \cite{cite_4}. To exploit these exotic flavours of spin and
valley in devices, it is necessary to actively control their electronic and magnetic properties at the nanoscale \cite{cite_15}.
